How We Tested Bike Locks

We evaluated each lock across four critical dimensions:

Cut Resistance

We tested each lock's resistance to bolt cutters across standard lengths (24-inch and 36-inch) and referenced published angle grinder test data. Shackle diameter, steel hardness, and geometry all factor into resistance. An 18mm shackle like the Fahgettaboudit is simply not cuttable with portable bolt cutters — physics wins.

Pick Resistance

We assessed cylinder quality, keyway design, and tolerances. Higher-quality cylinders with disc detainer or five-pin tumbler mechanisms are far harder to pick or shim than basic pin tumbler designs. We also checked for anti-rotation features that resist lever attacks.

Portability and Carry

We rode with each lock for multiple commutes, testing frame mounts, carrying options, and whether the lock impeded handling. A lock left at home protects nothing. Weight, pack size, and mounting solutions all affect whether you will actually carry it.

Ease of Use

Locking quickly is security in practice. We timed how long each lock took to secure a bike correctly, assessed keyway accessibility, and noted whether the lock mechanism works reliably in cold or wet conditions. Slow or awkward locks get left open.

Bike Lock Buyer's Guide

U-Lock vs. Chain Lock vs. Folding Lock vs. Cable Lock

Each lock type has a different security-to-portability trade-off:

  • U-locks are the best primary lock for most cyclists. Rigid steel shackle, compact, lightweight relative to security. Their rigidity resists leverage attacks and the small opening leaves little room for tools. Best choice for locking through the rear triangle to a fixed object.
  • Chain locks are the most flexible option. They lock around larger objects and through more complex frame layouts. Heavier per equivalent security level, but the flexibility makes them valuable as a secondary lock or in situations with non-standard locking points.
  • Folding locks balance flexibility and portability. They fold compactly onto the frame and open to provide chain-like flexibility. Security typically sits between a chain lock and U-lock. Ideal for cyclists who prioritize compact carry.
  • Cable locks are not serious security devices. A cable lock can be cut in seconds with any wire cutter. Use one only to secure accessories like helmets or as a third anchor point alongside a quality primary lock. Never rely on a cable as your only lock.

Security Ratings Explained

Third-party security certifications tell you what independent testers found — and they matter more than manufacturer claims:

  • Sold Secure Diamond — The UK's highest independent certification. Locks at this level resisted attack for the longest test window. Both the Fahgettaboudit Mini and Litelok X1 hold this rating.
  • Sold Secure Gold — Mid-tier certification, appropriate for most urban environments. Many excellent locks on this list hold Gold ratings.
  • Sold Secure Silver — Entry-level certification. Better than no certification, but only appropriate for low-risk areas or short-duration stops.
  • ABUS Security Level 1-15 — ABUS's own internal scale. Level 15 is their maximum. Useful for comparing ABUS products against each other, but less comparable to external brands than Sold Secure ratings.

Correct Locking Technique

Even the best bike lock is useless with poor technique. Follow these principles:

  • Lock through the frame, not just a wheel. A wheel-only lock lets a thief walk off with the frame.
  • Include the rear wheel in the lock loop when possible. The rear wheel is the most expensive wheel to replace.
  • Use a fixed, immovable anchor. Parking signs can be unscrewed; wooden posts can be cut. Look for dedicated bike racks or structural steel.
  • Minimize slack in the lock. More space inside the lock means more room for leverage tools. Fill as much of the lock's opening as possible with frame and rack.
  • Position the keyway facing down. This protects against drilling attacks and makes lock-picking harder.
  • Use two locks. Two different lock types force a thief to carry two different tools, dramatically increasing their time and exposure. See our cycling safety guide for more commuter security advice.

Insurance Requirements

If you plan to claim bike theft on insurance, your lock selection matters legally:

  • Most home contents policies require a specific security rating — typically Sold Secure Gold or above — for away-from-home theft coverage.
  • Some policies require a named approved lock. Check your policy schedule before assuming coverage exists.
  • Dedicated bicycle insurance (Velosurance, Markel, Spoke) generally requires Sold Secure Gold minimum for outdoor theft claims.
  • Keep receipts for your lock and photograph your bike with the serial number for any potential claim.
  • Kryptonite's Anti-Theft Protection Offer is a manufacturer guarantee, not insurance — it operates independently of your home policy and requires registration.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best bike lock for 2026?

The Kryptonite New York Fahgettaboudit Mini is the most secure bike lock for 2026. Its 18mm hardened steel shackle and Sold Secure Diamond rating make it virtually impervious to bolt cutters. For a balance of security and portability, the Kryptonite Evolution Mini-7 at $65 is the best mid-range U-lock for most cyclists.

Are U-locks better than chain locks?

U-locks and chain locks each have advantages. U-locks are more rigid, harder to attack with bolt cutters, and lighter per equivalent security level. Chain locks are more flexible, making it easier to lock to a wider variety of fixtures and secure multiple components. For most commuters, a quality U-lock is the better primary lock. A chain lock works well as a secondary lock or when flexibility is required.

What does Sold Secure Diamond rating mean?

Sold Secure is an independent UK security testing body. Diamond is their highest rating, meaning the lock resisted attack for the longest test duration using angle grinders, bolt cutters, and other tools. Gold is the mid-tier rating. Both the Kryptonite Fahgettaboudit Mini and Litelok X1 carry Sold Secure Diamond certification.

How should I lock my bike to prevent theft?

Always lock through the frame, not just the wheel. Secure to a fixed, immovable object. Position the lock keyway facing down to resist picking and drilling. Use two locks — a U-lock through the frame and rear wheel, plus a chain or cable through the front wheel. The harder you make theft, the more likely a thief will move on to an easier target.

Is the Kryptonite Fahgettaboudit worth the price?

Yes, for high-theft areas the Kryptonite New York Fahgettaboudit Mini at $110 is worth every dollar. Its 18mm hardened steel shackle cannot be cut with standard bolt cutters. If you own a bike worth $500 or more and park in an urban environment, the Fahgettaboudit is the most cost-effective security investment you can make.

What is the best folding bike lock?

The Abus Bordo Granit 6500 is the best folding bike lock for 2026. Its 5.5mm hardened steel bars fold into a compact package that mounts neatly on the frame. It earned a Sold Secure Gold rating and ABUS security level 15, making it one of the most secure folding locks available while remaining genuinely portable.

Do I need bike lock insurance?

Many home contents insurance policies cover bikes up to a certain value but typically require you to use an 'approved' lock. Sold Secure Gold or Diamond rated locks are widely accepted by insurers. Check your policy details. Dedicated bicycle insurance often requires specific lock ratings for away-from-home theft coverage.

How heavy is too heavy for a bike lock?

It depends on your riding style. Commuters on flat routes can tolerate a 4-5 lb lock like the Fahgettaboudit. Cyclists covering longer distances or hilly terrain often prefer sub-2 lb options like the Litelok X1 (1.8 lb) or Kryptonite Kryptolok (2.9 lb). The Abus Bordo Granit folds compactly at around 2.5 lb, making it a popular choice for riders who value portability.
