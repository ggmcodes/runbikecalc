Understanding Race Pace Relationships: 5K to Marathon Conversion Factors

Published: January 9, 2026•9 min read

You just ran a 22-minute 5K. What marathon time does that suggest? How much slower should your 10K pace be? This guide explains the mathematical relationships between race distances and how to use conversion factors effectively.

The Riegel Formula

Most race time predictors use a formula developed by Peter Riegel: T2 = T1 × (D2/D1)^1.06. The exponent (1.06) represents the slowing effect of longer distances. As distance increases, pace slows due to fatigue, glycogen depletion, and accumulated stress.

Example Calculations

Example: 22:00 5K Runner

  • 10K prediction: 22:00 × 2.09 = 45:59
  • Half marathon: 22:00 × 4.65 = 1:42:18
  • Marathon: 22:00 × 9.8 = 3:35:36

Why Predictions Aren't Always Accurate

Race predictors assume equivalent training and talent for each distance. Real-world factors that cause deviations:

  • Training specificity: A 5K specialist may underperform at marathon distance
  • Endurance development: Marathons require specific adaptations not needed for 5K
  • Experience: Pacing, fueling, and mental skills improve with distance experience
  • Body type: Some runners are naturally suited to different distances

Using Predictions Wisely

  1. Use recent race data: A race from 6+ months ago may not reflect current fitness
  2. Consider training context: Did you peak for that race? Were conditions ideal?
  3. Adjust for experience: First-time marathoners should add 5-10% to predictions
  4. Use multiple data points: Predictions from several races are more reliable

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert my 5K time to a marathon prediction?
A common formula multiplies your 5K time by 4.6-4.8 for marathon prediction. A 25-minute 5K predicts approximately 3:45-4:00 marathon. This assumes adequate marathon-specific training - without long run preparation, actual times will be slower.
Why does my predicted marathon time seem too fast?
Race predictors assume equivalent training for each distance. Most runners train more specifically for shorter races. Marathon performance depends heavily on endurance factors like glycogen management, pacing, and mental fortitude that aren't developed running 5Ks.
