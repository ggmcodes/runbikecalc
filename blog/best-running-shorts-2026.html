How We Evaluated Running Shorts

Good running shorts should disappear on your body—you shouldn't notice them at all. Here's the framework we used to evaluate each pair:

  • Fit and range of motion: Does the short restrict hip flexion or feel constricting during faster turnover? We evaluated user reports across easy pace and track intervals.
  • Chafing resistance: The inner thigh, waistband, and liner-to-skin interface are the three zones where shorts fail. We tracked chafe reports by location.
  • Moisture management: How quickly does the fabric shed sweat? Does it stay clingy after 60 minutes in 80+ degree heat, or does it stay dry and light?
  • Pocket bounce: User reports on loaded pockets (phone and gels) at tempo pace reveal how much the load disrupts stride.
  • Waistband comfort: Over 90+ minutes, tight or digging waistbands become painful. We assessed user feedback on contact pressure across long runs.
  • Durability and wash performance: After 20+ wash cycles, we checked user reports for fabric pilling, fading, and liner degradation.
  • Value: Does the price match the performance, comfort, and durability you're getting?

We evaluated performance across temperatures ranging from 45°F to 88°F, on roads, tracks, and light trails. Sweat rate, body type, and thigh size all affect chafing risk—we try to account for this by noting which shorts are better suited for runners who chafe easily versus those who don't.

How to Choose Running Shorts: The Complete Buyer's Guide

With eight strong options in this guide, the right short depends on your specific training needs, body type, and tolerance for chafing. Here's how to think through the decision.

Inseam Length: Which Is Right for You?

Inseam length is the first decision most runners need to make. Here's a practical breakdown:

  • 3-4 inch (split shorts): Maximum range of motion. Best for elite racers, track runners, and runners who prioritize stride freedom over coverage. Requires separate liner or brief underneath for most runners.
  • 5 inch: The sweet spot for most runners. Enough coverage to prevent inner thigh contact on most body types, without excess fabric. Our top recommended inseam length for daily training.
  • 6-7 inch: More coverage, better for runners with thicker thighs, those prone to chafing, or runners who simply prefer the feel of longer shorts. Slightly more fabric resistance at high speeds.

If you're unsure where to start, the 5-inch inseam is the right default. It's what the majority of competitive recreational runners wear, and it works for most body types without modification.

Liner Types: Brief vs. Compression vs. No Liner

The liner is often the difference between a comfortable run and a chafe-filled nightmare. Understanding the three main types helps you choose correctly:

  • Brief liner: A built-in underwear-style brief. The most common liner type. Works well for most runners on runs up to 2 hours. The Nike Dri-FIT Stride, Lululemon Fast and Free, and Under Armour Launch all use brief liners. The key quality indicator is the seam placement—flat-lock seams reduce skin abrasion significantly.
  • Compression liner: A tighter, more substantial inner short. Provides better thigh-on-thigh chafe protection and light muscle support. The Brooks Sherpa 5" 2-in-1 uses a full compression liner. Best for runners who chafe easily or who run marathon distances regularly. Warmer than a brief liner in hot conditions.
  • No liner (split shorts): Worn with compression shorts or briefs underneath, or alone by runners comfortable with the airflow. The Patagonia Strider Pro and Nike Aeroswift are liner-free split designs. Maximum breathability but requires planning your underlayer separately.

Pocket Placement and What Actually Works

Not all pockets are created equal. Here's what to look for based on what you carry:

  • Carrying a phone: You need a zippered side pocket large enough for your specific phone model. The Lululemon Fast and Free has the best phone pockets in our test. Side pockets distribute weight better than rear pockets for running gait stability.
  • Carrying gels: Side stash pockets (open-top) are fastest to access mid-run. Rear zip pockets hold gels securely but require you to reach behind your body. The Brooks Sherpa's four-pocket system is best for high-gel-count races.
  • Carrying a key only: Almost any rear zip pocket works fine. The single zip on the Nike Stride, New Balance Impact Run, and Under Armour Launch all handle a key without issues.
  • Racing without gear: No pockets needed — reach for the Nike Aeroswift or Patagonia Strider Pro.

Fabric Weight and Heat Management

Fabric weight directly affects how you perform in warm conditions. Lighter fabrics shed sweat faster and feel cooler when wet.

  • Ultra-lightweight (Nike Aeroswift, Patagonia Strider Pro): Best for hot weather and racing. Minimal thermal retention. Not as durable as heavier fabrics.
  • Standard lightweight (Nike Dri-FIT Stride, Rabbit EZ Short): The everyday training sweet spot. Wicks well and feels comfortable across a wide temperature range.
  • Mid-weight (Brooks Sherpa 2-in-1, Under Armour Launch): Slightly warmer, which means less ideal in 85°F+ conditions. The compression liner in the Sherpa adds meaningful thermal load in heat.

If you primarily run in summer heat, prioritize lighter-weight fabrics and look for mesh paneling. Our summer running heat training guide covers gear selection in detail alongside training adjustments for hot conditions.

Split Shorts vs. Lined Shorts: The Full Comparison

This is the question we get most often, especially from runners transitioning from lined shorts to splits. Here's an honest comparison:

Split Shorts Are Better For:

  • Racing and speed work
  • Maximum breathability in heat
  • Runners who already wear compression shorts
  • Track and 5K/10K distances
  • Minimalist gear philosophy

Lined Shorts Are Better For:

  • Daily training without separate underlayer
  • Casual and beginner runners
  • Long runs and marathon training
  • Runners who don't want to manage layering
  • All-in-one convenience

Most recreational runners are better served by lined shorts for training and can add a split short specifically for race days if they want the performance edge. You don't have to choose one or the other—our top recommendation is to own both.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the best running shorts in 2026?

The Nike Dri-FIT Stride 5" is the best running short for most runners in 2026, offering excellent moisture wicking, a smooth brief liner, and strong durability at $45. For racing and speed work, the Patagonia Strider Pro 5" is our top split-short recommendation. The Under Armour Launch 7" is the best budget option at $35.

What is the best inseam length for running shorts?

Most runners do best with a 5-inch inseam. It provides full range of motion without excess fabric or inner thigh chafing risk. Sprinters and racers often prefer 3-4 inch split shorts for maximum leg freedom. Runners who chafe easily or prefer more coverage often choose 6-7 inch options. When in doubt, start with 5 inches.

Split shorts vs. lined shorts — which is better for running?

Neither is universally better — they serve different purposes. Split shorts offer maximum freedom of movement and breathability, making them ideal for racing and speed work. Lined shorts are more convenient for daily training since they function as a complete garment without needing a separate brief. Most runners benefit from having both: lined shorts for training, a split short for race days.

Do I need running shorts with pockets?

If you run longer than 45 minutes regularly, pockets are worth having. A zippered rear pocket holds a key or gel on any length run. For carrying a phone or multiple gels on long runs, you want a short with multiple secure pockets — the Brooks Sherpa 5" 2-in-1 and Lululemon Fast and Free 6" are our top picks for pocket capacity. For short, fast runs and races, pockets can be omitted.

How do I prevent chafing in running shorts?

Choose shorts with seamless or flat-lock stitched liners, apply anti-chafe balm (Body Glide or Squirrel's Nut Butter) to inner thighs and waistband contact zones, and ensure your shorts fit snugly without bunching or riding up. Compression liners prevent thigh-on-thigh friction better than brief liners. The Rabbit EZ Short's ultra-soft fabric reduces skin irritation for sensitive runners. See our complete chafing prevention guide for more solutions.

What fabric should running shorts be made of?

The best running shorts use lightweight polyester or nylon blends with moisture-wicking technology. Look for four-way stretch for range of motion. Avoid cotton — it holds sweat and dramatically increases chafing risk. Nylon (like the Patagonia Strider Pro) is the lightest and most durable option. Polyester (like Dri-FIT) is excellent for moisture management at an accessible price point.

Are 2-in-1 running shorts worth it?

Yes, for most runners. The 2-in-1 design (outer shell plus built-in compression liner) eliminates the need for separate compression shorts, reduces thigh chafing, and provides more support on long runs. The Brooks Sherpa 5" 2-in-1 is our top pick in this category. The tradeoff is that 2-in-1 shorts run warmer than single-layer designs, so they're less ideal in extreme heat.

What running shorts do elite runners wear?

Elite runners typically wear ultra-lightweight split shorts with 3-4 inch inseams during races, including options like the Nike Aeroswift 4" Split or Patagonia Strider Pro. These minimize weight and maximize leg freedom during competition. In training, elites often rotate between split shorts for speed sessions and longer lined shorts or 2-in-1 designs for easy and long runs — the same rotation strategy that works for serious recreational runners.
