How We Evaluated

Each pedal system was evaluated across criteria that matter for the intended riding style:

  • Entry and exit feel: How intuitive is clipping in and out? Does engagement require precise foot placement or forgive slight misalignment?
  • Power transfer and platform stiffness: Is there any flex between shoe and pedal under hard efforts? Does the platform feel solid at threshold power?
  • Float and knee comfort: Does the cleat release tension remain consistent over weeks? Do riders report knee irritation that resolves with float adjustment?
  • Mud and weather performance: For MTB pedals, how did mud buildup affect entry? Did bearings corrode after wet rides without cleaning?
  • Walkability: For systems marketed as walkable, how do the cleats actually perform on pavement, tile, or dirt?
  • Bearing longevity: After 1,000+ miles, was bearing play developing? Did the manufacturer recommend re-greasing intervals?
  • Value: Does the performance justify the price compared to the rest of the field?

Cycling Pedal Buyer's Guide

Clipless vs Flat Pedals: Which Is Right for You?

Clipless pedals connect your shoe to the pedal via a mechanical cleat, allowing you to apply force through more of the pedal stroke — not just the downstroke, but also through the bottom and partially on the upstroke. Research on cycling efficiency consistently shows that clipless systems improve gross power output, particularly at high intensities. They also improve pedaling cadence consistency because your foot cannot slide on the pedal.

Flat pedals give you complete foot freedom. You can adjust your foot position mid-ride, step off instantly without an unclip motion, and use any shoe including casual footwear. In technical mountain biking, many expert riders prefer flat pedals because the ability to quickly get a foot down outweighs the power transfer benefits of clipless. Flat pedals also force better pedaling technique — if your technique is poor, clipless pedals mask it.

Our recommendation: Road and gravel cyclists should use clipless. Mountain bikers should try both — beginners on flat, progress to clipless if desired. Commuters do well with either SPD clipless or a quality hybrid pedal like the PD-EH500.

Look KEO vs SPD vs SPEEDPLAY: Cleat System Comparison

This is the most common question in road cycling, and the answer depends on your priorities.

  • Look KEO (and Shimano SPD-SL): 3-bolt cleat, large platform, excellent power transfer. Not walkable. Standard for road and triathlon. Use if you only ride on roads and performance is the priority.
  • SPD (Shimano 2-bolt): Small recessed cleat, walkable, compatible with all MTB shoes. Slightly smaller power transfer surface than road systems but negligible for most riders. Use if you commute, do gravel, or mountain bike.
  • SPEEDPLAY: Unique circular pedal with walkable cleat cover, dual-sided entry, and the most adjustable float available. The only road system that is genuinely walkable without a cleat cover you have to remember to attach. Use if you want road performance plus walkability.

Our verdict on SPD vs Look pedals: For pure road riding with road shoes, Look KEO offers wider platform contact and is easier to walk in than SPD-SL. SPD wins for any multi-surface or commuting application. SPEEDPLAY wins if walkability with road performance is your specific requirement.

Float Explained: How Much Do You Need?

Float is the amount of rotational movement your foot has within the cleat before it disengages. It is measured in degrees. Zero float means your foot is locked in a fixed position. Nine degrees of float means your foot can rotate 9 degrees before releasing.

Most cyclists should start with 6 degrees of float. This accommodates natural foot tracking variation (the slight angle change in foot position through the pedal stroke) without creating an unstable feeling. If you experience knee pain on the inside of your knee, try more float — your foot is likely being constrained in an awkward angle. If you want precise cleat placement for maximum power without movement, try less float after a professional bike fit.

Never start at zero float without a professional bike fit. Zero float with misaligned cleats is one of the leading causes of cycling knee injuries.

Cleat Compatibility: 2-Bolt vs 3-Bolt Shoes

Before buying pedals, check your shoes. Road cycling shoes have 3 bolt holes arranged in a triangular pattern and accept Look KEO, Shimano SPD-SL, or SPEEDPLAY cleats. Mountain bike and commuting shoes have 2 bolt holes in a parallel pattern and accept Shimano SPD, Crankbrothers, or other 2-bolt cleats.

Some shoes are designed with both 2-bolt and 3-bolt recessed mount options, making them compatible with either system. Check manufacturer specifications before purchasing. If you are buying cycling shoes alongside pedals, read our best cycling shoes guide to make sure your shoe and pedal systems match.

Q-Factor: Why Pedal Stance Width Matters

Q-factor is the lateral distance between the outside faces of the two pedal attachment points on your crank arms. A wider Q-factor positions your feet further apart on the bike. Most road bikes have a Q-factor between 146–150mm. Mountain bikes typically run 168–175mm.

Riders with wide hips or hip impingement issues sometimes benefit from a wider Q-factor (achieved through pedal axle spacers on some systems or by choosing pedals with longer axles). Conversely, riders with narrow hip width and knee tracking issues may want the narrowest Q-factor possible. Frequently Asked Questions

What are the best cycling pedals in 2026?

For road cycling, the Shimano Dura-Ace PD-R9100 is the top pick for performance and the Look Keo 2 Max is the best value. For mountain biking and commuting, the Shimano SPD PD-M520 is the most reliable and affordable clipless pedal. For flat pedals, the Race Face Chester is the best all-around choice.

Are clipless pedals better than flat pedals?

Clipless pedals improve pedaling efficiency by connecting your foot to the bike, allowing you to pull through the bottom of the pedal stroke. However, flat pedals are safer for beginners, better for technical mountain biking where you may need to dab a foot, and more convenient for commuting. Most road cyclists benefit from clipless. MTB riders often debate between the two — it comes down to riding style and terrain.

What is the difference between SPD and Look pedals?

SPD uses a small, recessed 2-bolt cleat that allows walking comfortably — it is the standard for mountain biking and commuting. Look KEO pedals use a larger 3-bolt cleat with a wide platform, optimized for road cycling efficiency. Look cleats are harder to walk in but offer a larger power transfer surface. Both are excellent systems; the right choice depends on your riding type and whether walkability matters to you.

What float setting should I use on clipless pedals?

Most riders should start with 6 degrees of float, which accommodates natural foot movement and reduces knee strain. If you experience knee pain, try more float. Competitive riders who want a locked feel may prefer less float. Never start with zero float without a professional bike fit — incorrect zero-float alignment is a leading cause of cycling knee injuries.

How do I know what cleat system to use?

Your cleat system is determined by your pedal and shoe combination. Road shoes have 3-bolt mounts (Look KEO, SPD-SL, SPEEDPLAY). MTB and commuting shoes have 2-bolt mounts (SPD, Crankbrothers). Check your shoe before buying pedals. If you are buying both together, choose your riding style first — road vs MTB vs commuting — then pick a matching pedal and shoe.

How long do cycling pedals last?

Quality clipless pedals typically last 5–10 years with proper maintenance. Bearings require periodic greasing, and cleats should be replaced every 5,000–10,000 miles or when release tension becomes inconsistent. Metal flat pedals can last indefinitely with pin replacement. Avoid riding through deep mud without cleaning and regreasing bearings afterward.

Can I use SPD pedals on a road bike?

Yes. Many road cyclists, especially commuters and gravel riders, use SPD pedals on road bikes. SPD cleats recess into the shoe sole, making walking much easier than Look or SPD-SL cleats. The trade-off is a smaller contact patch compared to road-specific systems, which may reduce peak power transfer slightly — but for most recreational riders, this difference is imperceptible.

What pedals are best for beginner cyclists?

Beginners should start with the Shimano SPD PD-M520 or the Shimano PD-EH500 hybrid pedal. The SPD system is easy to clip in and out, forgiving, and works with affordable cycling shoes. The EH500 has one flat side and one SPD side, letting you ride in regular shoes until you build confidence with clipping in and out at stops.
