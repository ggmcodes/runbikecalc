FTP: The Basics

Functional Threshold Power (FTP) represents the maximum power you can sustain for approximately one hour. It's used to:

  • Set training zones for structured workouts
  • Track fitness improvement over time
  • Compare performance to other cyclists (using W/kg)
  • Pace efforts in races and events

Raw FTP vs W/kg

Raw FTP in watts doesn't tell the whole story. A 70kg cyclist with 250W FTP has 3.57 W/kg, while an 85kg cyclist with 280W has only 3.29 W/kg. The lighter cyclist would climb faster despite lower absolute power.

Calculate your W/kg: FTP (watts) ÷ Body Weight (kg)

Realistic Improvement Expectations

  • Year 1: 20-50% improvement possible with consistent training
  • Year 2: 10-20% additional gains
  • Year 3-4: 5-10% annual improvement
  • Year 5+: 2-5% gains become harder to achieve

Genetics, age, training history, and available training time all affect your ultimate potential. Most recreational cyclists can reach 3.0-3.5 W/kg with dedicated training.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good FTP for a cyclist?
FTP varies widely by weight and training history. For recreational cyclists, 2.0-2.5 W/kg is typical. Well-trained amateurs reach 3.0-3.5 W/kg. Competitive cyclists often exceed 4.0 W/kg. Professional cyclists typically range from 5.5-6.5 W/kg.
How quickly can FTP improve?
New cyclists can see 20-50% FTP improvement in the first year. Experienced cyclists may improve 5-10% annually with focused training. Gains slow significantly after 3-4 years of training. Genetics ultimately determine your ceiling.
