What is Functional Threshold Power (FTP)?

Functional Threshold Power (FTP) is defined as the highest average power output a cyclist can sustain for approximately one hour. More technically, it represents the power output at your lactate threshold - the point where lactate accumulation in your blood begins to exceed your body's ability to clear it.

FTP is crucial for cyclists because it:

  • Establishes Training Zones: All power-based training zones are calculated as percentages of FTP
  • Tracks Fitness Progress: Changes in FTP indicate improvements or declines in fitness
  • Guides Race Strategy: Helps determine sustainable power outputs for different race durations
  • Optimizes Training: Ensures you're training at the correct intensities for specific adaptations

FTP vs. Other Power Metrics

  • FTP vs. Peak Power: FTP is sustainable power; peak power is maximum short-term output
  • FTP vs. VO2 Max Power: FTP is typically 75-85% of VO2 max power
  • FTP vs. Critical Power: FTP is more practical for training; critical power is more scientifically precise

Proper setup is crucial for accurate FTP testing

FTP Testing Methods

1. The 20-Minute Test (Most Popular)

The 20-minute test is the most widely used FTP testing protocol because it's practical and reasonably accurate.

Protocol:

  1. Warm-up (20 minutes):
    • 10 minutes easy spinning
    • 3 x 1-minute efforts at 100-110% estimated FTP (1 minute recovery)
    • 5 minutes easy spinning
  2. Test (20 minutes): Maximum sustainable effort for 20 minutes
  3. Cool-down (10-15 minutes): Easy spinning
  4. FTP Calculation: Average power √ó 0.95

Advantages:

  • Quick and practical
  • Easy to complete indoors
  • Widely accepted standard
  • Good for regular testing

Disadvantages:

  • Requires good pacing judgment
  • Can be mentally challenging
  • 0.95 multiplier may not be accurate for everyone

2. The Ramp Test

The ramp test is increasingly popular due to its simplicity and reduced mental stress.

Protocol:

  1. Warm-up (15 minutes): Easy spinning with a few short accelerations
  2. Test: Increase power by 20 watts every minute until exhaustion
  3. FTP Calculation: Maximum 1-minute power √ó 0.75

Advantages:

  • No pacing required
  • Less mentally demanding
  • Shorter duration
  • Good for beginners

Disadvantages:

  • May not suit all physiological profiles
  • Less specific to sustained efforts
  • Multiplier varies between individuals

3. The 8-Minute Test

A shorter alternative that's less mentally demanding than the 20-minute test.

Protocol:

  1. Warm-up (15-20 minutes): Progressive build with short efforts
  2. Test: 2 √ó 8-minute all-out efforts with 10 minutes recovery
  3. FTP Calculation: 90% of average power from both efforts

How to Prepare for FTP Testing

Training Preparation

  • Base Fitness: Build several weeks of consistent training
  • Recovery: Take 1-2 easy days before testing
  • Avoid Hard Training: No intense workouts 48 hours prior
  • Practice: Do a practice test to learn pacing

Day of Testing

  • Nutrition: Eat a normal meal 2-3 hours before
  • Hydration: Be well-hydrated but not over-hydrated
  • Equipment Check: Ensure power meter is calibrated
  • Environment: Control temperature and minimize distractions

Mental Preparation

  • Set Realistic Goals: Base expectations on recent training
  • Pacing Strategy: Plan your effort distribution
  • Motivation: Have music or entertainment ready
  • Acceptance: Some tests go better than others

Executing the Perfect FTP Test

20-Minute Test Pacing Strategy

  • Minutes 0-2: Start conservatively, 10-15 watts below target
  • Minutes 2-10: Settle into target power, find rhythm
  • Minutes 10-15: Maintain target, resist urge to increase
  • Minutes 15-18: Gradually increase if feeling strong
  • Minutes 18-20: Give everything you have left

Common Testing Mistakes

Starting Too Hard

The most common error is starting above sustainable power. This leads to rapid fatigue and lower overall average power.

Poor Pacing

Large power fluctuations reduce efficiency. Aim for steady, consistent power output throughout the test.

Inadequate Warm-up

Skipping or rushing the warm-up reduces power output and increases injury risk.

Environmental Factors

Testing in hot conditions, when dehydrated, or after poor sleep will negatively impact results.

Using Your FTP Results

Power Training Zones

FTP serves as the basis for all power-based training zones:

  • Zone 1 (Active Recovery): Less than 55% FTP
  • Zone 2 (Endurance): 56-75% FTP
  • Zone 3 (Tempo): 76-90% FTP
  • Zone 4 (Lactate Threshold): 91-105% FTP
  • Zone 5 (VO2 Max): 106-120% FTP
  • Zone 6 (Anaerobic Capacity): 121-150% FTP
  • Zone 7 (Neuromuscular Power): Over 150% FTP

Training Applications

Endurance Training

  • Base Miles: 65-75% FTP for aerobic development
  • Tempo Efforts: 76-90% FTP for sustained power
  • Sweet Spot: 84-97% FTP for efficient training

Threshold Training

  • FTP Intervals: 95-105% FTP for lactate threshold improvement
  • Over-Unders: Alternating above and below FTP
  • Sustained Efforts: Long intervals at FTP

When to Retest Your FTP

Frequency Guidelines

  • Beginners: Every 6-8 weeks
  • Intermediate: Every 8-12 weeks
  • Advanced: Every 12-16 weeks
  • In-Season: Monthly or before key events

Signs You Need to Retest

  • Workouts Feel Easy: Consistently hitting power targets with ease
  • Performance Improvements: Better race results or time trial performances
  • Training Block Completion: After 4-6 weeks of focused training
  • Seasonal Changes: Beginning of new training phases

Avoiding Overesting

Too frequent testing can be counterproductive:

  • Physical Stress: FTP tests are demanding and require recovery
  • Mental Fatigue: Constant testing can reduce motivation
  • Minor Fluctuations: Day-to-day variations don't reflect true fitness changes

Factors Affecting FTP Test Results

Physiological Factors

  • Fitness Level: Higher fitness = higher FTP
  • Body Weight: Affects power-to-weight ratio
  • Training History: Experience with sustained efforts
  • Muscle Fiber Type: Slow-twitch favors FTP performance

Environmental Factors

  • Temperature: Heat reduces power output
  • Altitude: Higher altitude decreases FTP
  • Humidity: High humidity impairs performance
  • Air Quality: Pollution affects oxygen availability

Equipment Factors

  • Power Meter Accuracy: Different meters may read differently
  • Bike Position: Aerodynamics vs. power output
  • Trainer Resistance: Indoor trainers may affect pacing

Advanced FTP Testing Concepts

Durability vs. FTP

Recent research suggests that the ability to sustain power over time (durability) is as important as absolute FTP for endurance performance.

FTP Variants

  • Outdoor FTP: Often 5-10% higher than indoor due to cooling and biomechanical differences
  • Climb-Specific FTP: May differ from flat road FTP
  • Time Trial FTP: Aerodynamic position may affect sustainable power

Using FTP with Other Metrics

  • W/kg Ratio: Power-to-weight for climbing performance
  • TTE (Time to Exhaustion): How long you can sustain FTP
  • VLaMax: Anaerobic power production capacity

Conclusion

FTP testing is an essential skill for any cyclist serious about power-based training. Whether you choose the 20-minute test, ramp test, or another protocol, the key is consistency in your approach and using the results to guide your training decisions.

Remember that FTP is just one metric in your training arsenal. Use it in conjunction with perceived effort, heart rate, and performance indicators to get a complete picture of your fitness and progress.
