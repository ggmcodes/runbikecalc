Aerobic vs Anaerobic Training: When and How to Use Each

Published: December 10, 2024 • 9 min read • By RunBikeCalc Team

Every endurance athlete relies on both aerobic and anaerobic energy systems, but understanding when and how to train each system is crucial for optimal performance. While aerobic training builds the foundation for endurance activities, anaerobic training develops the power and speed needed for high-intensity efforts. This comprehensive guide explains the science behind both systems and provides practical strategies for balancing them in your training program.

Understanding Energy Systems

Your body produces energy (ATP) through three primary systems, each dominating at different intensities and durations:

The Three Energy Systems

1. Phosphocreatine System (Immediate)

  • Duration: 0-10 seconds
  • Intensity: Maximum effort
  • Fuel: Stored phosphocreatine
  • Examples: Sprints, jumps, explosive movements

2. Anaerobic Glycolytic System

  • Duration: 10 seconds - 2 minutes
  • Intensity: Very high (85-100% max)
  • Fuel: Glucose without oxygen
  • Examples: 400-800m runs, hard cycling climbs

3. Aerobic System

  • Duration: 2+ minutes to hours
  • Intensity: Low to moderate (<85% max)
  • Fuel: Carbohydrates and fats with oxygen
  • Examples: Marathon running, long cycling, easy training

Sprinting relies primarily on anaerobic energy systems

Aerobic Training: The Foundation

What is Aerobic Training?

Aerobic training occurs when your body uses oxygen to produce energy for sustained activity. This system is efficient and can utilize both carbohydrates and fats as fuel sources.

Key Characteristics of Aerobic Training

  • Sustainable Pace: Can be maintained for extended periods
  • Lower Heart Rate: Typically 50-80% of maximum
  • Conversational Effort: Able to speak during exercise
  • Primary Fuel: Mix of carbohydrates and fats
  • Low Lactate: Minimal lactate accumulation

Benefits of Aerobic Training

Physiological Adaptations:

  • Increased Mitochondria: More cellular powerhouses for energy production
  • Enhanced Capillarization: Better oxygen delivery to muscles
  • Improved Fat Oxidation: Better utilization of fat for fuel
  • Stronger Heart: Increased stroke volume and cardiac output
  • Better Oxygen Extraction: Muscles become more efficient

Performance Benefits:

  • Endurance Base: Foundation for all endurance sports
  • Faster Recovery: Between workouts and within sessions
  • Injury Prevention: Lower impact stress on body
  • Mental Resilience: Builds patience and consistency

Types of Aerobic Training

Zone 2 Training (Base Aerobic)

  • Intensity: 60-70% max HR
  • Duration: 45 minutes to several hours
  • Purpose: Build aerobic base
  • Frequency: 3-6 times per week

Tempo Runs (Threshold Aerobic)

  • Intensity: 80-85% max HR
  • Duration: 20-60 minutes
  • Purpose: Improve lactate clearance
  • Frequency: 1-2 times per week

Long Slow Distance

  • Intensity: 65-75% max HR
  • Duration: 90+ minutes
  • Purpose: Build endurance and mental toughness
  • Frequency: Once per week

⚡ Energy System Contribution by Duration

Short Duration

  • • 10 seconds: 90% anaerobic, 10% aerobic
  • • 30 seconds: 80% anaerobic, 20% aerobic
  • • 60 seconds: 70% anaerobic, 30% aerobic
  • • 2 minutes: 50% anaerobic, 50% aerobic

Long Duration

  • • 5 minutes: 20% anaerobic, 80% aerobic
  • • 10 minutes: 10% anaerobic, 90% aerobic
  • • 30 minutes: 5% anaerobic, 95% aerobic
  • • 60+ minutes: 2% anaerobic, 98% aerobic

Anaerobic Training: Power and Speed

What is Anaerobic Training?

Anaerobic training occurs when energy demands exceed what can be met through oxygen-dependent pathways. This system is powerful but produces lactate and other metabolic byproducts that limit duration.

Key Characteristics of Anaerobic Training

  • High Intensity: 85-100% of maximum effort
  • Short Duration: Seconds to a few minutes
  • High Heart Rate: Near maximum
  • Lactate Production: Rapid accumulation
  • Quick Fatigue: Cannot be sustained long

Benefits of Anaerobic Training

Physiological Adaptations:

  • Increased Power: Greater force production capacity
  • Enhanced Buffering: Better tolerance to lactate and acidity
  • Neuromuscular Improvements: Better muscle recruitment
  • Glycolytic Enzymes: Improved anaerobic energy production
  • Fast-Twitch Development: Enhanced explosive capabilities

Performance Benefits:

  • Speed Development: Faster race paces
  • Finishing Kick: Strong sprint at race end
  • Hill Power: Better climbing ability
  • Race Tactics: Ability to respond to surges

Types of Anaerobic Training

VO2 Max Intervals

  • Intensity: 90-100% max HR
  • Work Duration: 3-8 minutes
  • Rest Ratio: 1:1 or less
  • Example: 5 × 4 minutes at 5K pace

Lactate Tolerance Training

  • Intensity: 95-105% of threshold
  • Work Duration: 30 seconds - 3 minutes
  • Rest Ratio: 1:0.5 to 1:1
  • Example: 8 × 400m at mile pace

Neuromuscular Power

  • Intensity: Maximum effort
  • Work Duration: 10-30 seconds
  • Rest Ratio: 1:5 or more
  • Example: 10 × 100m sprints

High-intensity intervals develop anaerobic power

Balancing Aerobic and Anaerobic Training

The 80/20 Rule

Research suggests most endurance athletes benefit from:

  • 80% Low Intensity: Aerobic, conversational pace
  • 20% High Intensity: Anaerobic, hard efforts

This distribution provides optimal adaptation while minimizing injury risk and overtraining.

Sport-Specific Considerations

Marathon Running

  • Aerobic: 85-90% of training
  • Anaerobic: 10-15% of training
  • Focus: Aerobic capacity and efficiency

5K/10K Running

  • Aerobic: 75-80% of training
  • Anaerobic: 20-25% of training
  • Focus: Balance of speed and endurance

Sprint Events

  • Aerobic: 40-50% of training
  • Anaerobic: 50-60% of training
  • Focus: Power and speed development

Periodization: Timing Your Training

Base Phase (Aerobic Focus)

  • Duration: 8-16 weeks
  • Aerobic: 90-95% of training
  • Anaerobic: 5-10% of training
  • Purpose: Build foundation

Build Phase (Balanced)

  • Duration: 6-10 weeks
  • Aerobic: 70-80% of training
  • Anaerobic: 20-30% of training
  • Purpose: Develop race-specific fitness

Peak Phase (Race Preparation)

  • Duration: 3-4 weeks
  • Aerobic: 60-70% of training
  • Anaerobic: 30-40% of training
  • Purpose: Sharpen for competition

Common Training Mistakes

Too Much Anaerobic Training

Many athletes train too hard too often:

  • Problem: Chronic fatigue and plateaus
  • Signs: Declining performance, frequent illness
  • Solution: Increase easy aerobic volume

Neglecting Anaerobic Development

Some athletes avoid high-intensity work:

  • Problem: Lack of speed and power
  • Signs: Good endurance but slow race times
  • Solution: Add structured speed work

Gray Zone Training

Training in the middle ground between systems:

  • Problem: Not easy enough for aerobic benefits
  • Issue: Not hard enough for anaerobic gains
  • Solution: Polarize training intensities

⚠️ Training Balance Considerations

  • • Individual response to training varies significantly
  • • Recovery capacity affects optimal anaerobic volume
  • • Age and training history influence ideal balance
  • • Seasonal variations may require adjustments
  • • Monitor fatigue and adjust accordingly

Monitoring Training Balance

Key Indicators

Aerobic Development:

  • Resting Heart Rate: Lower indicates better aerobic fitness
  • Heart Rate Drift: Less drift during long efforts
  • Recovery Rate: Faster return to baseline
  • Pace at HR: Faster speeds at same heart rate

Anaerobic Development:

  • Peak Power: Higher maximum outputs
  • Lactate Tolerance: Longer time at high intensity
  • Sprint Speed: Faster short efforts
  • Recovery Between Intervals: Quicker readiness

Training Metrics

  • Training Impulse (TRIMP): Quantifies training load
  • Time in Zones: Track distribution across intensities
  • Chronic Training Load: Long-term training stress
  • Acute:Chronic Ratio: Fatigue vs fitness balance

Sample Weekly Training Plans

Endurance Athlete (Marathon/Long Distance)

  • Monday: Rest or easy recovery (aerobic)
  • Tuesday: Tempo run 30-45 min (threshold aerobic)
  • Wednesday: Easy run 60 min (aerobic)
  • Thursday: VO2 max intervals (anaerobic)
  • Friday: Recovery run 30 min (aerobic)
  • Saturday: Long run 90-150 min (aerobic)
  • Sunday: Easy run 45-60 min (aerobic)

Middle Distance Athlete (5K/10K)

  • Monday: Recovery run 30 min (aerobic)
  • Tuesday: Track intervals at 5K pace (anaerobic)
  • Wednesday: Easy run 45 min (aerobic)
  • Thursday: Tempo run 20-30 min (threshold)
  • Friday: Rest or easy 20 min
  • Saturday: Long run with surges (mixed)
  • Sunday: Recovery run 40 min (aerobic)

Nutrition for Different Energy Systems

Aerobic Training Nutrition

  • Pre-Workout: Light meal 2-3 hours before
  • During: Water for efforts under 90 minutes
  • Fuel Sources: Balanced carbs and fats
  • Recovery: Moderate protein and carbs

Anaerobic Training Nutrition

  • Pre-Workout: Carbohydrate focus 1-2 hours before
  • During: Quick carbs for extended sessions
  • Fuel Sources: Primarily carbohydrates
  • Recovery: Immediate carbs and protein

Conclusion

Understanding the differences between aerobic and anaerobic training systems is fundamental to designing an effective endurance training program. While aerobic training builds the foundation for all endurance activities, anaerobic training provides the speed and power needed for optimal performance.

The key is finding the right balance for your specific sport, goals, and individual physiology. For most endurance athletes, the 80/20 principle provides an excellent starting point, with adjustments based on your event demands and training phase.

Remember that both systems are important and work synergistically. A strong aerobic base supports better anaerobic performance, while anaerobic training can improve your aerobic capacity.
