Understanding Flat Feet: A Medical Perspective

Flat feet, medically known as pes planus, is a condition where the arch of the foot collapses, causing the entire sole to make contact with the ground. This affects approximately 20-30% of the general population and can be either flexible (the arch is visible when not bearing weight) or rigid (the arch is absent regardless of weight-bearing status).

Types of Flat Feet

Flexible Flat Feet

The most common type. The arch appears when sitting but flattens when standing. Many people with flexible flat feet are asymptomatic and may not need specialized footwear. However, runners often benefit from stability features to prevent excessive motion during the gait cycle.

Rigid Flat Feet

A less common but more problematic condition where the arch is absent in all positions. This type often requires more aggressive intervention including motion control shoes, custom orthotics, or in severe cases, surgical correction. Runners with rigid flat feet should consult a podiatrist before beginning a running program.

The Biomechanics of Flat Feet During Running

When you run, your foot goes through a complex cycle of pronation (rolling inward) and supination (rolling outward). The arch acts as a natural shock absorber and helps transfer energy efficiently during push-off. Without a functional arch:

  • Excessive pronation: The foot continues to roll inward past the normal range, stressing the medial (inner) structures of the foot, ankle, and knee.
  • Reduced shock absorption: Ground reaction forces are transmitted more directly through the skeletal system rather than being dissipated by the arch.
  • Inefficient push-off: Energy that should be stored and released by the arch is lost, potentially affecting running economy.
  • Compensatory movements: The body may compensate by altering hip, knee, or ankle mechanics, potentially leading to injuries up the kinetic chain.

Common Injuries Associated with Flat Feet

Runners with flat feet may be at increased risk for certain overuse injuries:

Posterior Tibial Tendinitis

Inflammation of the tendon that supports the arch. Symptoms include pain and swelling along the inner ankle and foot. Early treatment is crucial to prevent progression to adult-acquired flatfoot deformity.

Plantar Fasciitis

While often associated with high arches, plantar fasciitis also affects flat-footed runners due to increased tension on the plantar fascia when the arch collapses.

Shin Splints

Medial tibial stress syndrome is more common in overpronators due to increased stress on the tibialis posterior and other lower leg muscles.

Knee Pain (Patellofemoral Syndrome)

Overpronation can cause internal rotation of the tibia, altering knee tracking and increasing risk of patellofemoral pain syndrome.

Medical Disclaimer

This guide is for informational purposes only. If you experience persistent pain or have concerns about your foot structure, consult a podiatrist or sports medicine physician. They can provide personalized recommendations based on your specific anatomy and biomechanics.

What to Look for in Running Shoes for Flat Feet

When shopping for running shoes with flat feet, focus on these key features:

1. Medial Post or GuideRails

The defining feature of stability shoes. A medial post is a firmer section of foam on the inner midsole that resists excessive inward rolling. Brooks' GuideRails technology takes a different approach, using rails that extend from the heel to guide the entire lower leg rather than just the foot. Both approaches are effective; personal preference matters more than the specific technology.

2. Firm but Responsive Midsole

An overly soft midsole can exacerbate pronation issues. Look for shoes with responsive foam that provides cushioning without excessive collapse. Modern midsole foams like Brooks DNA LOFT, HOKA's CMEVA, or Saucony's PWRRUN offer a balance of comfort and support.

3. Wide Base of Support

A wider outsole platform increases stability. HOKA shoes excel here with their wider-than-average platforms, providing a stable foundation even with their maximalist cushioning.

4. Structured Heel Counter

A firm heel counter helps control rearfoot motion during initial ground contact. Test this by pressing on the heel cup - it should resist deformation. However, modern stability shoes don't require the rigid "cup" design of older models.

5. Adequate Arch Support

While stability features are important, don't overlook the insole. Many stability shoes come with removable insoles that can be replaced with custom orthotics if needed. The stock insole should provide noticeable arch support without feeling intrusive.

6. Appropriate Heel-to-Toe Drop

Most flat-footed runners do well with moderate heel drops (8-12mm), which encourage a heel strike pattern and provide good proprioceptive feedback. Very low drops can increase strain on the Achilles tendon in overpronators.

Exercises to Strengthen Flat Feet

While proper footwear is essential, strengthening the muscles that support your arch can improve function and reduce injury risk. Incorporate these exercises into your routine 3-4 times per week:

Towel Scrunches

Place a towel flat on the floor. Using only your toes, scrunch the towel toward you. Repeat 10-15 times per foot. This strengthens the intrinsic foot muscles that support the arch.

Short Foot Exercise

While sitting, try to raise your arch by pulling the ball of your foot toward your heel without curling your toes. Hold 5 seconds, repeat 10 times. Progress to standing once comfortable.

Calf Raises

Standing on a step with heels hanging off, rise onto your toes then lower slowly. Perform 15-20 reps. This strengthens the posterior tibial tendon and calf muscles that support arch function.

Toe Yoga

Practice lifting your big toe while keeping the other toes down, then reverse. This improves foot control and strengthens muscles involved in arch support.

Marble Pickup

Place marbles on the floor and pick them up one at a time with your toes, placing them in a cup. Do 20 marbles per foot. Great for toe dexterity and arch strength.

Single-Leg Balance

Stand on one foot for 30-60 seconds, focusing on keeping your arch engaged. Progress by closing your eyes or standing on an unstable surface.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the best running shoes for flat feet in 2026?

The Brooks Adrenaline GTS 24 is the best overall running shoe for flat feet in 2026. It provides excellent stability through GuideRails technology without feeling overly rigid. For maximum support with cushioning, the HOKA Gaviota 5 is excellent. The Saucony Guide 18 offers great value at $150.

Do I need stability shoes if I have flat feet?

Not necessarily. While many people with flat feet overpronate, some have flexible flat feet that function normally. A gait analysis can help determine your needs. Signs you may need stability shoes include excessive shoe wear on the inside edge, ankle or knee pain after running, or visible ankle rolling.

What causes flat feet?

Flat feet can be congenital or develop over time due to aging, injury, obesity, diabetes, or pregnancy. The posterior tibial tendon can weaken, leading to fallen arches. About 20-30% of adults have flat feet, though not all experience problems.

What is overpronation and how does it relate to flat feet?

Overpronation occurs when your foot rolls inward excessively during running. Flat feet often lead to overpronation because the collapsed arch doesn't properly control foot motion. Stability shoes use medial posts or guide rails to limit this inward rolling.

Can running make flat feet worse?

Running itself doesn't cause flat feet, but running without proper support can exacerbate symptoms. Proper footwear, arch support, and strengthening exercises can actually help manage flat feet. Many flat-footed runners complete marathons successfully when properly equipped.

Should I use orthotics with stability running shoes?

It depends on your needs. Many runners find stability shoes sufficient alone. However, severe overpronation or structural issues may benefit from custom orthotics. If using orthotics, consider neutral shoes to avoid overcorrection.

How do I know if I have flat feet?

Try the wet test: wet your feet and stand on paper. If your footprint shows your entire sole with no curve inside, you likely have flat feet. You can also check if your ankles lean inward when standing. For definitive diagnosis, see a podiatrist.

Are motion control shoes better than stability shoes for flat feet?

Motion control shoes offer more rigid support and are designed for severe overpronation. However, most runners with flat feet do well with stability shoes, which balance support and natural motion. Motion control is best reserved for very heavy overpronators or those with specific medical needs.
