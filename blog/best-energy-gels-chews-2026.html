The difference between finishing strong and hitting the wall almost always comes down to nutrition strategy and product choice. For this guide, we evaluated every gel and chew listed using expert reviews, nutritional lab data, and extensive marathon runner feedback — covering everything from 20-mile long runs to full marathons.

Each product was evaluated on taste, texture, stomach tolerance, energy delivery, and portability. I ran with the exact protocols I'd use on race day: gels every 35-40 minutes, taken with water, starting at the 30-minute mark. This isn't a summary of label claims — it's what these products actually felt like at mile 18 when your body is screaming for fuel.

I also consulted research on carbohydrate absorption rates and GI tolerance in endurance sport to ground these real-world observations in science. What works in a lab test and what works mid-race are sometimes different things, and I've tried to bridge that gap in every review below.

How We Evaluated Energy Gels and Chews

We evaluated each product across five criteria that actually matter on race day:

  • Taste at fatigue: A gel that tastes fine at mile 3 may be unbearable at mile 20. We evaluated palatability reports from fatigued athletes, not just fresh off the starting line.
  • Texture and ease of consumption: Can you get it down at race pace without slowing to a walk? Does it require excessive chewing or leave a coating in your mouth?
  • Stomach tolerance: Did it cause nausea, bloating, or GI distress? We evaluated user reports with water only (standard race conditions) and without water to understand how the formulas perform under stress.
  • Energy delivery: Subjective assessment of the energy kick — how fast did it hit, how long did it last, and did it produce any noticeable crash?
  • Portability and packaging: Does the wrapper open easily with sweaty hands? Does it fit in a running vest or shorts pocket without leaking?

Products that failed the stomach tolerance test under normal conditions were noted clearly. Any gel can upset your stomach if you use it without training — these are observations from runners who regularly practice fueling in training.

Maurten's Gel 100 is the most scientifically interesting product on this list. Its hydrogel polymer technology encapsulates 25g of carbohydrates in a matrix that behaves as a low-osmolality gel in the gut — meaning it doesn't pull water into your intestines the way high-concentration traditional gels can. This is the main reason elite marathoners running sub-2:10 pace rely on it: at that intensity, GI distress is career-ending.

Testing Maurten at slower training paces, the difference is subtler but still real. I noticed a distinct absence of the slight "stomach weight" I sometimes feel with thicker gels during 20-milers. The texture is somewhere between water and a soft gel — it slides down with almost no effort and requires no water to consume comfortably, though I still recommend taking it with a small sip.

The GU vs Maurten debate is one of the most common questions in the endurance nutrition world. The honest answer: for recreational runners doing their first or fifth marathon at comfortable paces, GU works just as well at less than half the price. Maurten's edge appears at high intensities (sub-3-hour marathon effort and faster) where GI stress is amplified and every calorie absorbed matters more.

At $3.75 per gel, Maurten is genuinely expensive. Used across a full marathon fueling plan, that's $22+ in gels per race. Complete Buyer's Guide: How to Choose Your Marathon Fuel

Gel vs Chew vs Waffle: Which Format is Right for You?

Energy Gels

Best for road racing, shorter efforts, and runners who want a fast, low-effort fuel hit.

  • • Fastest to consume
  • • No chewing required
  • • Compact and pocketable
  • • Requires water (most formulas)

Energy Chews

Best for ultra runners, trail runners, and anyone who prefers spreading fuel over time.

  • • Flexible partial servings
  • • More food-like experience
  • • Slower consumption
  • • Great for long slow efforts

Waffles & Real Food

Best for ultra runners, long training runs, and anyone with a tired stomach.

  • • Feels like actual food
  • • Best palatability after 3+ hours
  • • Higher calorie density
  • • Less portable at race pace

Fueling Timing: When to Take Your First Gel

The most common fueling mistake is waiting too long. Your glycogen stores begin depleting from the moment you start running. Most sports scientists recommend starting fuel intake at 30-45 minutes into an effort lasting more than 60-75 minutes — don't wait until you feel depleted, because by then absorption takes time and you may never fully recover.

Sample Marathon Fueling Timeline

  • Mile 5 (35 min): First gel or 2 chews. Begin before you feel hungry.
  • Mile 9 (1:05): Second gel. Stay ahead of depletion.
  • Mile 13 (1:35): Third gel. Consider switching to caffeinated.
  • Mile 17 (2:05): Fourth gel. Critical fueling window before miles 18-20.
  • Mile 21 (2:35): Fifth gel if needed. Gut tolerance varies here.

Caffeine Strategy for Endurance Events

Caffeine is one of the most researched ergogenic aids in endurance sport. A dose of 3-6mg per kg of body weight has been shown to improve endurance performance by 2-4%. For a 150-pound runner, that's roughly 200-400mg of caffeine — more than you'd want to front-load, but achievable by saving caffeinated gels for the back half of a race.

A common protocol: use non-caffeinated gels for miles 1-13, then switch to caffeinated gels (GU's 40mg, SiS's 75mg, or Clif Bloks' 50mg per serving) for miles 14 through the finish. This times the caffeine peak to land during the hardest miles. Never try a caffeinated gel for the first time on race day — practice in at least 2-3 long training runs.

Stomach Training: Why Race-Day Fueling Must Be Practiced

Your gut is a trainable organ. Runners who practice taking gels during long training runs develop better GI tolerance on race day. Research shows that consistent carbohydrate ingestion during training increases the expression of intestinal carbohydrate transporters, literally increasing your body's absorption capacity over time.

If you're new to gels, start with one gel per long run and gradually increase. Don't take a new brand or flavor for the first time on race day. Check your race nutrition calculator to dial in your exact hourly carb targets based on race distance and pace.

Gel Tips for Beginners

Always take gels with 4-6 oz of water. Never take multiple gels back to back. Practice your exact race-day fuel protocol in your 3 longest training runs. Start with mild flavors like vanilla or plain.

Hydration Pairing

Don't take gels with sports drink — the combined carbohydrate concentration can overwhelm your gut. Take gels with water only, and take your electrolyte drink separately. See our best electrolytes guide for the right pairing.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best energy gel for running in 2026?

GU Energy Original is the best energy gel for most runners in 2026. It delivers 100 calories, 450mg of amino acids, and comes in 20+ flavors at roughly $1.50 per gel. For elite runners or those with sensitive stomachs, Maurten Gel 100 is worth the premium price.

How often should I take an energy gel during a marathon?

Most runners take a gel every 30-45 minutes during a marathon, starting at the 30-45 minute mark. This delivers 30-60g of carbohydrates per hour, which aligns with the body's absorption capacity for most trained athletes. Always take gels with water, not sports drink, to avoid over-concentrating your gut.

GU vs Maurten: which is better?

GU Energy is better for most runners due to variety, affordability, and proven performance across all distances. Maurten Gel 100 is better for runners with sensitive GI systems or those racing at sub-3-hour marathon pace, where its hydrogel technology provides faster absorption with less GI distress. The price difference is significant: GU costs about $1.50/gel vs Maurten's $3.75/gel.

Are energy chews better than gels?

Neither is universally better — it comes down to personal preference. Gels are faster to consume and easier to digest on the run. Chews like Clif Bloks require chewing, which some runners find uncomfortable at race pace, but others prefer the food-like texture and the ability to take partial servings. Try both in training before committing to one format for race day.

Can energy gels upset your stomach?

Yes, energy gels can cause GI distress if you don't practice with them in training, take them without water, or consume too many too fast. Always train with the exact gel you plan to race with, take gels with 4-6 oz of water, and start fueling earlier rather than waiting until you're depleted.

Do I need caffeine gels for marathon running?

Caffeine in energy gels (typically 20-100mg) can improve performance by 2-4% in endurance events. Most runners benefit from saving caffeinated gels for miles 16-20 of a marathon for a late-race boost. Avoid caffeine gels if you're caffeine-sensitive or haven't trained with them before race day.

What is the best real food energy gel for running?

Spring Energy Awesome Sauce is the best real food gel for runners who want whole-food ingredients. It uses brown rice, beet, and banana for 200 calories per packet. Honey Stinger Organic Waffles are another excellent whole-food option for runners who prefer solid nutrition during long efforts, especially trail and ultra distances.

How many carbs should I take per hour during a long run?

Research recommends 30-60g of carbohydrates per hour for runs over 75 minutes, and up to 90g per hour for efforts over 2.5 hours using multiple carb sources (glucose + fructose). Most energy gels provide 20-30g of carbs, so you'll typically need 2-3 gels per hour for higher-end protocols. Always build up carb intake gradually in training to adapt your gut.
