Understanding Cycling Glove Technology

Why Gel Padding Matters

Gel padding is strategically placed in cycling gloves to protect the ulnar and median nerves in your palms. These nerves run close to the skin surface and are compressed during riding, causing numbness, tingling, and pain on long rides.

Premium gloves like the Castelli Arenberg Gel 2 use dual-density gel pads that distribute pressure evenly while maintaining bar feel. The gel absorbs vibrations from rough roads and reduces peak pressure points that cause nerve compression.

Gel vs. Foam Padding

Gel Padding: Superior pressure distribution and vibration damping. Ideal for rides over 2 hours or rough roads. Slightly heavier and less breathable.

Foam Padding: Lighter and more breathable. Better for short rides in hot weather. Less effective at preventing numbness on long rides.

Sizing and Fit Tips

Proper fit is crucial for comfort and safety:

  • • Measure hand circumference at widest point (excluding thumb)
  • • Gloves should be snug but not restrictive
  • • Fingers shouldn't have excess material bunching
  • • Palm padding should align with pressure points
  • • For fingerless gloves, cuff shouldn't restrict wrist flexion
  • • Castelli runs small—size up if between sizes
  • • Giro tends to run true to size

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I really need cycling gloves?

Yes. Cycling gloves provide crucial hand protection in crashes, reduce pressure points and numbness on long rides, improve grip on sweaty handlebars, and protect hands from weather. Even short rides benefit from the comfort and safety they provide.

What's the difference between gel and foam padding?

Gel padding offers superior vibration damping and pressure distribution, making it ideal for long rides and rough roads. Foam padding is lighter and more breathable, better for short rides in hot weather. Premium gloves like the Castelli Arenberg Gel 2 use strategic gel placement for the best of both worlds.

Should I buy short finger or long finger cycling gloves?

Short finger (fingerless) gloves work best above 60°F for maximum ventilation and bar feel. Long finger gloves protect from cold, wind, and road debris. Many cyclists own both types and choose based on conditions. For year-round riding, consider getting one of each.

How should cycling gloves fit?

Snug but not tight. The gloves should move with your hand without bunching or creating pressure points. For fingerless gloves, the cuff shouldn't restrict wrist movement. For full-finger winter gloves, ensure fingertips aren't cramped and you can still operate brake levers comfortably.

How do I wash cycling gloves?

Hand wash in cold water with mild detergent. Avoid machine washing which can damage gel padding and seams. Air dry completely—never use a dryer as heat breaks down gel and elastic materials. Wash after every 2-3 rides to prevent bacteria buildup and odor.
